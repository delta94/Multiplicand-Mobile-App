In calculus, an antiderivative or indefinite integral of a function \(f\) is a differentiable function F whose derivative is equal to the original function \(f\). This can be stated symbolically as \({\displaystyle F'=f}\). The process of solving for antiderivatives is called antidifferentiation (or indefinite integration) and its opposite operation is called differentiation, which is the process of finding a derivative.

Antiderivatives are related to definite integrals through the fundamental theorem of calculus: the definite integral of a function over an interval is equal to the difference between the values of an antiderivative evaluated at the endpoints of the interval.

Given a function, \(f(x)\), anti-derivative of \(f(x)\) is any function \(F(x)\) such that :
\(F'(x) = f(x)\)
\(F(x)\) is any anti-derivative of \(f(x)\) then the most general anti-derivative of \(f(x)\) is called indefinite integral and denoted,
\(\int f(x)dx = F(x) + c ,\) \(c\) is any constant

Properties

  • \(\int k.f(x)dx\) \(=\) \(k.\int f(x)dx\) where \(k\) is any number. So, we can factor multiplicative constants out of indefinite integrals.
  • \(\int -f(x)dx\) \(=\) \(-\int f(x)dx\) This is really the first property with \(k= -1\)
  • \(\int f(x) \pm g(x) dx = \int f(x) dx \pm \int g(x)dx\). In other words, the integral of a sum or difference of functions is the sum or difference of the individual integrals. This rule can be extended to as many functions as we need.
  • When integrating multiple times, certain additional techniques can be used, see for instance double integrals and polar coordinates, the Jacobian and the Stokes' theorem

Techniques of integration

Finding antiderivatives of elementary functions is often considerably harder than finding their derivatives. For some elementary functions, it is impossible to find an antiderivative in terms of other elementary functions. See the articles on elementary functions and nonelementary integral for further information.

  1. The linearity of integration breaks complicated integrals into simpler ones
  2. Integration by substitution, often combined with trigonometric identities or the natural logarithm
  3. Integration by parts to integrate products of functions
  4. Inverse function integration, a formula that expresses the antiderivative of the inverse \(f^{-1}\) of an invertible and continuous function \(f\) in terms of the antiderivative of \(f\) and of \(f^{-1}\).
  5. The method of partial fractions in integration allows us to integrate all rational functions (fractions of two polynomials)
